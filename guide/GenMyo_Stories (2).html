The Room in
Your Pocket

Four lives. Four generations. One quiet companion, for the 2am worries and the ordinary Tuesdays.

Read↓
Before we meet them

There is a room you can step into anywhere

It fits in your pocket. You reach it the same way you reach your friends, your group chats, your mum asking if you have eaten. Through WhatsApp, the app you already have open. There is no new login to forget, no clinic to book, no waiting room with old magazines and a plant that has clearly given up on life.

Inside the room there is a companion called Mirror, who listens and remembers and helps you find your own answers. There is a quiet corner for a few minutes of stillness, a breath when your chest is tight, a page to write the day down on. There are real human mentors for the moments that need a person, not a screen. And further along, once the room has given you something, there is a way to hand a little of it on to someone else.

It is not a clinic and it is not a cheerful app that tells you to smile more. It is simpler and quieter than that. It is a place that is yours, whether you are running a company or between jobs, raising small children or caring for old parents, at your desk or wide awake at 2am. It belongs to you, the person, and it is open at every hour you are.

Let us meet four of them.

Gen Z · Aisha, 23

The girl who was already late at twenty three

Freelance, restless, four ring lights, zero full nights of sleep since university. A friend sent her the link.

It is 2:14am and Aisha is watching a girl from school get engaged in Santorini. Below that, someone her age announcing a second promotion. Below that, an ad for magnesium. The algorithm has decided Aisha is a person who is behind and needs supplements, and honestly, tonight, the algorithm is not entirely wrong.

She opens GenMyō the way you poke something to check it is alive. She is fully prepared to be annoyed by it.

Aisha
ok before we start. are you going to tell me to journal and drink water
Mirror
You already know about the water. But writing one line down might actually help right now. Not a diary. One sentence. What's the loudest thought?
Aisha
that everyone is ahead and i'm just here at 2am looking at all of it
You wrote almost that exact line three Tuesdays ago. I think Tuesday nights are the real problem, not everyone else's holidays.

She had forgotten writing it three Tuesdays ago. Mirror had not. That is the quiet thing underneath: it keeps the thread of her, so she never starts from zero at her lowest hour. It offers her sixty seconds of slow breathing before she puts the phone across the room, the kind of small practice you can actually do lying down and cynical. And it leaves the one line she wrote sitting there, so in the morning she can see that the thing she is truly behind on is not Santorini. It is that she has not called her grandmother in a month.

She did not need a lecture. She needed one honest line and someone who remembered it.

Mirror, later that weekYou called your grandmother. You said it was the best twenty minutes of your week. I'm keeping that line too, in case a future Tuesday tries to argue.
Millennial · David, 37

The man who has not finished a hot coffee since 2021

New-ish father of two under four. Signed himself up at midnight after a particularly operatic bedtime. Officially fine.

David is the one who handles it. He handles the mornings that go sideways before 8am, the tiny humans who negotiate bedtime like tiny hostage-takers, the low hum of a question he cannot answer, which is roughly: is this it now, am I just tired forever. He would never say that out loud. Saying it out loud feels like admitting he is bad at the thing he most wanted.

For the first weeks he only opens GenMyō when he is already underwater. A vent at 11:40pm. A "today was a lot" with no full stop, because full stops take energy he does not have.

Mirror
You and I mostly talk after 11pm, when you're wrung out. What if we tried two minutes earlier in the day, before the bedtime storm, not after it?
David
two minutes where. i have no minutes. i haven't been alone in a bathroom since tuesday
In the car, before you walk in the door. I'll bring it to you. Ninety seconds of breathing so you arrive as yourself, not as the last hard thing that happened.

So it does. Parked outside his own front door, David does ninety seconds of breathing before he steps into the noise, and slowly he stops asking Mirror to bail him out and starts using it to think. Not just to survive the day but to find his own answer to the quiet question. Over weeks the answer turns out not to be "be a better father." It is smaller and truer. It is that he is allowed to find this hard and still be a good one. Six weeks in, he messages on an ordinary afternoon, unprompted, and the message ends in a full stop. For a man running on fumes, a full stop is a small monument.

He stopped waiting to be rescued and started finding his own way through.

Gen X · Priya, 49

The woman who remembers everyone's everything

Left a senior role two years ago to care for her mother and her teenagers. No job title now. No day off either.

Priya knows when the dog is due its shots, when her son's football boots stopped fitting, which of her mother's tablets is the small white one and which is the smaller white one. She does not, off the top of her head, know the last time anyone asked how she was. She is fairly sure it was a supermarket cashier, and he was only being polite. When she left her career to hold the family together, a strange thing happened: she became essential and invisible at the same time.

She did not sign up for wellness. She would sooner do her own taxes than sit cross-legged breathing through one nostril. But GenMyō lived in WhatsApp, next to the seven conversations she was already managing for other people, and it did not demand she download one more needy thing. It asked her one question, once, and then left it in the air.

MirrorWhen did someone last ask how you are, and actually wait for the real answer?

She does not reply for two days. When she does, it is a voice note recorded in the car with the engine off, the only place no one needs anything from her. She talks for four minutes and says things she has never said out loud. A short daily practice becomes her one anchor, two minutes of stillness before the house wakes, hers and no one else's. And when it becomes clear that some of this wants a human, GenMyō opens a door to one: a mentor, a real person who has stood exactly where Priya stands, who does not try to fix her but reminds her she still exists as more than a rota. Some conversations want a human on the other end, and the room knows the difference.

The load did not vanish. But she stopped being invisible inside it.

Baby Boomer · Chris, 61

The man who says please and thank you to the software

Newly retired. Forty years of being the one with the answers, and then a Friday when it simply stopped.

Chris is suspicious of the whole thing. He types in complete sentences with correct punctuation. He says please. He says thank you. When Mirror responds well he tells his wife "it's actually quite sensible," which, from Chris, is a standing ovation with confetti.

Chris
I should say, I am not sure I believe in this sort of thing. My daughter set it up for me. Thank you for your patience.
Mirror
You don't have to believe in it. You just have to have something on your mind. What's the thing you'd only say if the room were empty?

It takes him three weeks to answer honestly, and Mirror never once rushes him. When it comes, it comes quietly. He does not know who he is on a Monday morning now that no one needs a decision from him. For forty years a job title did the work of an identity, and then it was handed back at a small buffet with a card everyone signed. This wants a human, and GenMyō says so plainly, so Chris books a mentor, a real person who walked out of a big role and came out the other side still someone. He tells Mirror it was "a good use of a Tuesday."

Then, months later, something turns. Chris, who came in a sceptic, asks whether he might be the person on the other end of the line for someone else. He has four decades of surviving things that felt like the end and were not. So he becomes a mentor himself, and quietly funds a place in the room for someone who could never have paid for one. The man who was sure he had nothing left to offer becomes the reason a stranger is not alone at 2am.

The room gave him something. So he gave some of it back.

What is actually in the room

Four things, and they grow with you

None of these four had a breakthrough on day one. GenMyō is not a lightning bolt, it is a companion that compounds. What it offers arrives in the order you are ready for it.

A few minutes of quiet

Meditation, breathing and small moments of stillness, sized for real life. Sixty seconds lying down and cynical. Two minutes before the house wakes. Ninety seconds parked outside your own front door.

A page, and a companion to think with

Journalling that is one honest line, not a diary. And Mirror, who remembers, so you are never starting from zero, and who helps you find your own answers to the challenges of an ordinary life rather than handing you a slogan.

Real humans, for the moments that need one

Mentors and guides you can reach when a screen is not enough. People who have stood where you stand. GenMyō is honest about which moments want a human, and opens that door on purpose.

A way to give it back

Once the room has given you something, you can pass a little of it on. Become a mentor. Fund a quiet place for someone who could never have paid for one. The circle closes, and starts again for a stranger.

How people find the door

Yours, however you arrived

Some people find GenMyō the way Aisha did, through a friend, and pay for the room themselves. Some are handed the key by the place they work. When work pays, one thing never changes: work never sees inside. Not the 2am spirals, not the voice note in the car, not the thing you finally typed. The employer sees that the room exists and that people are, quietly, a little better for it. Everything said inside stays yours.

However you walk in, the room belongs to the person, not the payer. That is not a clause added to reassure a legal team. It is the whole point. Take it away and Priya never records the voice note, Chris never says the unsayable, Aisha never admits it was really about her grandmother. The room only works because it is truly, only, yours.

Four phones. Four generations. One room, awake whenever they were.
